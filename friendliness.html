How to Be Friendly & Warm With Others

Friendliness isn’t just a trait people are born with—it’s a set of behaviors and attitudes you can practice and get better at. This article from Science of People offers 10 actionable steps to help you appear more approachable, build instant rapport, and deepen your connections.

Start With the Right Mindset

Before your words or body even come into play, your internal mindset sets the tone. Adopting friendliness as a default attitude helps you act more genuine, confident, and receptive to others.

  1. Assume rapport — act as if others already like you.
  2. Become genuinely interested in others rather than trying to impress them.
  3. Turn off the "spotlight effect" — realize people aren’t focused on your every stumble.

Show Friendliness Through Nonverbal Signals

Before you speak, your body language and eye behavior communicate a lot. Use subtle nonverbal cues to express warmth and connection.

  1. The 3-Second Gaze — hold eye contact long enough to show attention, not awkwardness.
  2. Eyebrow Flash — a quick raise signals recognition and openness.
  3. Lean In (vs slouch back) — a slight forward lean shows engagement.

Lead With Warmth & Conversation Starters

Once your nonverbal "door" is open, what you say next can either invite deeper connection or stall the moment. Use low-risk compliments, curiosity, and conversational generosity to keep things smooth.

  1. Offer a low-risk compliment (e.g. about their choice of book or style).
  2. Use a “conversation spark” — a small personal thought or observation to open dialogue.
  3. Become conversationally generous — let them shine, ask open-ended questions.
  4. Go deeper than “What do you do?” — ask about stories, values, or passions.

Stay Friendly, Even Over Text & Email

The digital world steals tone — but you can still radiate warmth. Use smiles, friendly sign-offs, and a conversational voice to make your messages inviting.

  1. Begin with warm greetings, not blunt “hey.”
  2. Mirror tone and pacing (don’t be too formal if they’re casual).
  3. Use occasional exclamations or emojis (judiciously) to soften the tone.

Final Takeaways

Building friendliness is about combining mindset, nonverbal cues, and thoughtful speech. The more you practice these small habits—assuming rapport, making eye contact, offering warmth, asking deeper questions—the more natural being “friendly you” becomes. It’s not about being perfect. It’s about being open, kind, and genuinely curious about others.
